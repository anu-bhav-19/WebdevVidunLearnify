The Ontario Parks system stretches over 125 years. It began in 1893 with the creation of Algonquin Provincial Park. In 1954 the management and creation of provincial parks came under the Department of Lands and Forests.There are now 330 provincial parks comprising 8% of Ontario's landmass which is an area larger (over 82,000 km2) than the Provinces of Nova Scotia and PEI combined.

History and Organization

Bruce Peninsula National Park

Bruce Peninsula National Park, established in 1987, is a stunning natural area in Ontario that showcases rugged cliffs, clear turquoise waters, and diverse ecosystems. This park is part of the Niagara Escarpment and serves as a critical habitat for many species, while also offering breathtaking views of the Great Lakes.

Kejimkujik National Park

Kejimkujik National Park in Nova Scotia is a beautiful park known for its rich Mi'kmaq history and extensive network of lakes and rivers, making it an ideal spot for canoeing, hiking, and camping. The park features ancient petroglyphs and offers visitors a glimpse into the cultural heritage of the Indigenous peoples of the region.

Point Pelee National Park

Point Pelee National Park is a unique coastal park located on a peninsula that juts into Lake Erie in Ontario, Canada. This park is recognized for its diverse habitats, including marshes, forests, and beaches, making it an important stopover for migratory birds and a haven for wildlife enthusiasts.

Fundy National Park

Fundy National Park is renowned for having the highest tides in the world, located along the Bay of Fundy in New Brunswick. The park encompasses lush forests, dramatic cliffs, and scenic coastal views, providing numerous opportunities for hiking, camping, and observing unique tidal ecosystems

Kawartha Highlands Provincial Park

Kawartha Highlands Provincial Park, established in 1989, is the largest park in southern Ontario after Algonquin Provincial Park. This natural environment park features a stunning array of lakes, wetlands, and forests, offering visitors a tranquil escape for activities like canoeing, hiking, and camping amidst its diverse ecosystems.

Safety and Regulations

Fire Regulations: Fire bans may be in place during dry conditions. Always check for current fire regulations and use designated fire pits.

Wildlife: Parks are home to bears, moose, and other wildlife. Follow park guidelines for food storage and encounters.

Permits: Backcountry camping and fishing require permits, which can be obtained at park offices or online.

Classification of Parks

Parks are classified into various categories based on their purpose, management, and ecological significance. These classifications help in the effective conservation and management of natural resources. Common types include national parks, which preserve large natural areas for public enjoyment and ecological integrity; state parks, which offer recreational opportunities and protect local ecosystems; and urban parks, designed to provide green spaces within city environments. Each category serves distinct functions, from preserving biodiversity to offering recreational activities, ensuring that parks meet the diverse needs of communities while safeguarding the environment.

North America's Boreal Forest
Songbird Nursery

The Great Migration

Every spring, billions of birds embark on an incredible journey northward to Ontario's boreal forests. These forests, protected by Ontario Provincial Parks, serve as a crucial summer home for countless songbird species.

The long days and abundant food sources make Ontario's boreal region an ideal nursery for raising young birds.

A Thriving Ecosystem

Ontario's boreal forests offer a rich and diverse ecosystem that supports a wide variety of songbirds. The abundance of insects, berries, and seeds provides the perfect environment for birds to feed their growing chicks.

Provincial parks play a crucial role in preserving these habitats, ensuring the continued success of migratory songbirds.

Conservation Efforts

Ontario Provincial Parks are at the forefront of songbird conservation. By protecting large swaths of boreal forest, these parks provide safe breeding grounds for millions of birds each year.

Ongoing research and monitoring programs help park managers understand and protect the needs of these important migratory species.
